Studying Euclidean geometry in foundational mathematics is a tradition that dates back to the Ancient greeks. As a nod to this tradition, we explore the material in the following sections.

Points, Lines, & Planes

Points. In geometry, a point is a location. It has no dimensions — i.e., no width, length, or depth. We represent a point with a dot. We generally denote points with capital letters. For example, the point ${A,}$ among others:

Line Segments. With two points, we can draw a straight path from one point to the other, forming a line segment. The two points at the ends of the line segment are called endpoints. With points ${A}$ and ${B,}$ we denote the line segment between the two points ${\overline{\rm AB}.}$

Note, however, that the endpoints are not the only points on the line segment. A line segment contains infinitely many points. The endpoints are just two special points indicating the line segment's ends. A third special point is the midpoint, the point exactly halfway between the endpoints. This means the midpoint is the same distance from both endpoints. We express this fact when we say that the midpoint is equidistant from the endpoints. We use tick marks to indicate that line segments are of equal length, using multiple tick marks for multiple line segments of equal length.
